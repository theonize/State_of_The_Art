# 22 · The Unity &amp; Division of the Church

<p class="epigraph">"…that all of them may be one… so that the world may believe that you have sent me." — John 17:21</p>

On the night before he died, Jesus prayed — not that his Church would be
powerful, or right about everything, or culturally dominant, but that it would be
**one**, so that the world might believe. Measured against that prayer, the
state of the Church is a scandal: tens of thousands of denominations, congregations
splitting over personalities and politics, and a watching world that sees not
one body but a thousand warring factions waving the same Bible.

And yet division is not the whole story. Beneath the fractures there remains
**one Lord, one faith, one baptism** — a unity that is already ours in Christ and
that we are called to make visible. This chapter faces the scandal of our
divisions honestly, distinguishes healthy diversity from sinful schism, and asks
how we might pursue the unity Jesus prayed for, in both truth and love.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On the unity of the Church">

We declare that **the Church is one in Christ** — one Lord, one faith, one
baptism — and that this unity is both a gift we already have and a task we are
called to make visible. We confess that our divisions are a scandal to the gospel
and grieve the heart of Christ, and we commit to pursue unity **in truth and in
love**. We renounce both sectarian pride that splits over secondary things and a
false "unity" that abandons the truth.

<Scripture passage="John 17:20-23" /> <Scripture passage="Ephesians 4:4-6" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

The Church has never been more fragmented — or more globally vast. Denominations
multiply; congregations divide over worship styles, politics, and personalities;
and online tribalism trains believers to treat fellow Christians as enemies. At
the same time, the faith is exploding across the global South, and ancient
traditions — Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant, Pentecostal — together still confess
the same crucified and risen Lord.

Two errors keep us from the unity Jesus prayed for. One is **sectarian pride**:
treating our tradition, our leader, or our secondary convictions as the boundary
of the true Church, and splitting at the slightest difference. The other is a
**truth-less false unity**: papering over real and important differences as if
nothing matters. The way of Christ is harder and better than either — to hold
fast to the truth *and* to love, honor, and seek visible fellowship with all who
are truly his.

## What Scripture says

Jesus' own prayer ties the Church's unity to the world's belief.

<Scripture passage="John 17:20-23" />

There is a unity that simply *is* — grounded in one God, one Lord, one Spirit,
one faith, one baptism — which we are to guard, not manufacture.

<Scripture passage="Ephesians 4:3-6" />

Division along party lines ("I follow Paul"; "I follow Apollos") is rebuked as
worldly and immature.

<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 1:10-13" /> <Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 3:3-7" />

The body of Christ has many different members, and its diversity is a strength,
not a defect — the eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you."

<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 12:12-26" />

And the world is meant to recognize us not by our uniformity but by our love.

<Scripture passage="John 13:34-35" />

## Discern

All Christians long for unity; they differ on what kind of unity to seek and how
to pursue it.

<Views title="How should we pursue the Church's unity?">
<View label="Visible / structural unity" open>

Seeks the healing of actual divisions — shared confession, sacraments, and
recognition between churches (the ecumenical impulse). *Caution:* unity must be
in the truth, not merely organizational merger that buries real differences.

</View>
<View label="Spiritual unity and cooperation">

Emphasizes the real, invisible unity of all true believers across traditions,
expressed through cooperation in mission and love rather than structural merger.
*Caution:* "invisible" unity must still become visible enough for the world to
see (John 17), not an excuse for endless division.

</View>
<View label="Unity in truth first">

Insists that genuine unity must rest on shared truth, so faithfulness to the
gospel cannot be sacrificed for the sake of togetherness. *Caution:* must
distinguish gospel essentials from secondary matters (see
[Heresy & Discernment](/issues/heresy)), lest it baptize needless division.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip The old, wise rule
Most of our divisions come from confusing the essential with the secondary. The
ancient counsel still heals: **in essentials, unity; in non-essentials, liberty;
in all things, charity.** Before you break fellowship, ask honestly whether the
issue is the gospel itself — or only your preference dressed as principle.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="Why does the Church's unity matter so much to Jesus?">

Because he tied it to the world's belief: "that all of them may be one… so that
the world may believe that you have sent me" (<Scripture passage="John 17:21" />).
Our unity is meant to be visible evidence of the gospel's truth, and our love for
one another the mark by which the world knows we are his
(<Scripture passage="John 13:35" />). Division therefore wounds not only the body
but the Church's witness.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="What is the difference between healthy diversity and sinful division?">

The body of Christ has many different members and gifts — diversity is a strength
God designed (<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 12:12-26" />). **Division**
becomes sinful when it splits the body over personalities, politics, or secondary
matters, or sacrifices truth for the sake of a faction ("I follow Paul… I follow
Apollos," <Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 1:10-13" />). The test is whether the
issue touches the gospel itself or merely our preferences.

</SelfCheck>
