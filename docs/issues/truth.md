# 15 · Truth, Misinformation &amp; the Digital Age

<p class="epigraph">"Therefore each of you must put off falsehood and speak truthfully to your neighbor, for we are all members of one body." — Ephesians 4:25</p>

We live in the first era in which any person can broadcast anything to the whole
world in seconds — true or false, kind or cruel — and in which machines can
manufacture convincing words, voices, and faces that were never real. The result
is an information crisis: a flood of misinformation, conspiracy, slander, and
outrage, in which many have simply stopped believing that truth is knowable at
all. This is not only a technological problem. It is a spiritual one, because the
God we worship calls himself **the Truth**, and the enemy he opposes is **the
father of lies**.

The Church has a peculiar calling here, and a peculiar temptation. The calling:
to be a community of truth and integrity in a lying age. The temptation: to
spread the falsehoods that flatter our side, to believe the conspiracy that
confirms our fears, and to bear false witness against our opponents while
congratulating ourselves on our "discernment." How we handle truth — especially
about people we dislike — is a test of whether we belong to the God of truth.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On truth and our witness">

We declare that **God is true**, that Jesus is the Truth, and that his people are
called to truthfulness and integrity in word and deed. We renounce lying,
slander, gossip, conspiracy, and the careless spread of falsehood — **especially
against our neighbors and our opponents** — and we refuse to traffic in lies even
when they serve our cause. We commit to speak the truth in love, to test claims
with humility, and to be known as people whose word can be trusted.

<Scripture passage="Ephesians 4:25" /> <Scripture passage="John 8:44" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

The digital age has rewired how we know things. Algorithms reward outrage over
accuracy, the sensational lie travels faster than the sober truth, and we each
curate echo chambers that confirm what we already believe. Conspiracy theories —
some of them genuinely dangerous — have spread widely, at times *inside* the
Church, dressed up as spiritual discernment. And now generative AI can fabricate
"evidence" — deepfake voices and videos — that erodes our last assumption that
seeing is believing.

Two opposite failures tempt us. One is **gullibility**: believing and forwarding
whatever fits our fears or our politics, without ever checking. The other is
**cynicism**: concluding that since everyone lies, nothing is true and nothing
can be trusted. Scripture calls us past both — to a confident, humble pursuit of
truth, anchored in the God who is faithful and cannot lie.

## What Scripture says

Truthful speech is not optional for God's people; lying assaults the very body we
belong to.

<Scripture passage="Ephesians 4:25" />

The ninth commandment forbids bearing false witness — a sin we commit not only in
courtrooms but in group chats and comment threads.

<Scripture passage="Exodus 20:16" /> <Scripture passage="Proverbs 19:9" />

Jesus identifies himself with truth and traces lies to their source; to love lies
is to follow another father.

<Scripture passage="John 14:6" /> <Scripture passage="John 8:44" />

The tongue — and now the keyboard — holds the power of life and death, and God
hates the sowing of discord and false reports.

<Scripture passage="James 3:5-10" /> <Scripture passage="Proverbs 6:16-19" />

So we are to test everything, holding on to the good, rather than believing every
spirit or every story.

<Scripture passage="1 Thessalonians 5:21" /> <Scripture passage="1 John 4:1" />

## Discern

Christians agree we must be truthful; they differ on the best practices for living
wisely in a polluted information ecosystem.

<Views title="How should Christians steward truth online?">
<View label="Rigorous discernment" open>

Develop the habits of careful thinking: check sources, distrust outrage, refuse
to share what you have not verified, and hold beliefs in proportion to evidence.
*Caution:* discernment is humble inquiry, not the conspiracist's "I've done my
own research" that trusts only fringe voices.

</View>
<View label="Digital simplicity">

Recognize that the medium forms us, and deliberately limit it — fasting from
feeds, slowing down, choosing fewer and better sources, protecting attention and
peace. *Caution:* withdrawal can become a way to avoid loving real neighbors in
the public square.

</View>
<View label="Truthful presence">

Stay engaged, but as a non-anxious, truthful presence — correcting falsehood
gently, refusing to dehumanize, modeling a better way to disagree. *Caution:*
"engagement" must not become addiction to being right or to the dopamine of
the fight.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip The hardest test of honesty
Misinformation spreads fastest when it flatters us. So apply the test where it
costs: *Would I fact-check this as carefully if it made my own side look bad?
Am I willing to believe an inconvenient truth, and to disbelieve a convenient
lie?* People of the Truth follow the evidence even when it wounds their tribe.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="Why is misinformation a spiritual issue and not just a technical one?">

Because God is the God of truth — Jesus calls himself "the truth"
(<Scripture passage="John 14:6" />) — and lies are traced to the enemy, "the
father of lies" (<Scripture passage="John 8:44" />). To traffic in falsehood,
slander, and conspiracy is not a neutral habit; it aligns us against the
character of God and breaks the ninth commandment
(<Scripture passage="Exodus 20:16" />). Truthfulness is part of holiness.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="What are the two opposite errors to avoid in the information age?">

**Gullibility** — believing and spreading whatever confirms our fears or
politics without checking — and **cynicism** — concluding that since everyone
lies, nothing is true or knowable. Scripture calls us to a third way: a humble,
confident pursuit of truth that "tests everything" and holds to the good
(<Scripture passage="1 Thessalonians 5:21" />), anchored in a God who cannot lie.

</SelfCheck>
