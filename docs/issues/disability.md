# 27 · Disability &amp; the Church

<p class="epigraph">"On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable." — 1 Corinthians 12:22</p>

Roughly one in six people lives with a significant disability — and yet people
with disabilities are among the most absent from our congregations, often kept
away not by their bodies but by our buildings, our assumptions, and our
discomfort. The Church has frequently treated disability as a problem to be pitied
or a ministry to be done *to* people, rather than recognizing those it too easily
overlooks as **full image-bearers** and **indispensable members** of the body of
Christ.

This chapter declares that dignity is never a matter of ability, that the body of
Christ is incomplete without its members the world calls "weak," and that the
goal is not merely access but **belonging** — friendship, gifts received, and a
place at the table that no one has to earn.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On disability and belonging">

We declare that **people with disabilities fully bear the image of God** and
possess full, God-given dignity — not based on ability or usefulness — and that
they are **indispensable members of the body of Christ**, not objects of pity or
mere recipients of charity. We commit to pursue not just access but genuine
belonging, receiving their gifts and friendship. We renounce the exclusion,
condescension, and "usefulness" logic that treats any life as worth less.

<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 12:22-25" /> <Scripture passage="Luke 14:13-14" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

For many people with disabilities, the barrier to church is not the gospel but
the gap at the threshold — stairs without ramps, services unintelligible to the
deaf or neurodivergent, programs with no place for them, and congregations that
mean well but do not know how to welcome. Where access exists, belonging often
does not: people are present but not befriended, served but not invited to serve.

A darker logic runs beneath the culture: that a life's value tracks its
capacities, so that disability is a tragedy to be prevented — a logic now visible
in the steep decline of births of children with conditions like Down syndrome
(see [Life & Death](/issues/life-and-death)). Scripture confronts this directly.
It says the parts of the body we deem "weaker" are *indispensable*, that God's
power is made perfect in weakness, and that the King's banquet is filled
precisely with those who cannot repay.

## What Scripture says

The body of Christ does not merely tolerate its "weaker" members; it cannot do
without them.

<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 12:21-25" />

Jesus tells us to fill the table with exactly those the world overlooks — and
blesses us *because* they cannot repay.

<Scripture passage="Luke 14:12-14" />

God's power is made perfect in weakness, overturning our rankings of strength.

<Scripture passage="2 Corinthians 12:9-10" />

And the dignity of every person rests on creation, not capacity — even our frailty
is known and made by God.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 139:13-14" /> <Scripture passage="Exodus 4:11" />

## Discern

Christians agree on full belonging; they emphasize different paths toward it (and
hold the question of healing humbly).

<Views title="How do we move toward genuine belonging?">
<View label="Access and welcome" open>

Remove the practical barriers — physical, sensory, cognitive — so that worship
and community are truly open to all. *Caution:* access is the threshold, not the
destination; presence is not yet belonging.

</View>
<View label="Belonging and friendship">

Move beyond accommodation to real relationship — friendship, inclusion in the
ordinary life of the church, and receiving people's gifts and leadership.
*Caution:* avoid tokenism; belonging means being needed, not merely permitted.

</View>
<View label="Holding healing humbly">

Scripture shows Jesus healing, and we may rightly pray for healing — yet a
person's worth and God's image in them never depend on being "fixed," and many
faithful saints live with lifelong disability. *Caution:* never imply that
unhealed disability is a failure of faith.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip Not "to," but "with" and "from"
The shift the gospel asks is from ministry *to* people with disabilities, to
ministry *with* them — and even *from* them. The body that excludes its "weaker"
members is not strong; it is incomplete, missing parts Scripture calls
indispensable (<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 12:22" />).
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="How does Scripture value those the world calls 'weak'?">

It calls them **indispensable**. Paul says the parts of the body that "seem to be
weaker are indispensable" and are to be given special honor
(<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 12:22-25" />). Jesus fills his banquet with
those who cannot repay (<Scripture passage="Luke 14:13-14" />), and God's power is
"made perfect in weakness" (<Scripture passage="2 Corinthians 12:9" />). Dignity
rests on creation in God's image, never on capacity or usefulness.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="What is the difference between access and belonging?">

**Access** removes the practical barriers so people *can* be present — ramps,
interpretation, accommodations. **Belonging** goes further: real friendship,
inclusion in the ordinary life of the church, and receiving people's gifts and
leadership. A congregation can be fully accessible and still leave people
unknown and unneeded. The body of Christ is meant for belonging, where the
"weaker" members are honored as indispensable, not merely admitted.

</SelfCheck>
