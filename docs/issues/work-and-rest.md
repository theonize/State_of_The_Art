# 19 · Work, Rest &amp; Sabbath

<p class="epigraph">"The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath." — Mark 2:27</p>

Most of our waking lives are spent working — or worrying about work, or
recovering from it. So how we understand work and rest shapes almost everything.
Our age has managed to get both wrong at once: it has made an **idol** of work,
measuring human worth by productivity and output, while hollowing out the
**rest** that keeps us human. The result is an epidemic of burnout, and a quiet
suspicion that we are only as valuable as what we produce.

Scripture tells a better story. Work is a **good gift**, woven into our creation —
a way to serve God and neighbor and to share in God's own creativity. And rest is
not laziness or weakness but a **command and a grace** — a weekly declaration that
we are creatures, not machines, and that the world is held by God, not by us.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On work and rest">

We declare that **work is a good gift and a calling** — all honest labor has
dignity, not only "religious" work — and that **rest is both a command and a
grace**, because we are creatures, not machines. We declare that **our worth is
not our productivity** but is given by God. We commit to honor the worker and the
unemployed alike, and we renounce both the idolatry of work that consumes us and
the contempt for ordinary labor and laborers.

<Scripture passage="Genesis 2:15" /> <Scripture passage="Exodus 20:8-10" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

The modern economy never sleeps, and our devices have erased the boundary between
labor and life — work follows us to the dinner table, the bed, the vacation.
"Hustle culture" preaches that rest must be earned and that to stop is to fall
behind. Many are exhausted, anxious, and unable to switch off; others, shut out
of meaningful work, feel discarded and worthless. Beneath both lies the same lie:
that a person's value rises and falls with their output.

The Church is not immune — it can sanctify overwork as "sacrifice," burn out its
people in the name of ministry, and quietly rank lives by usefulness. The
biblical rhythm of work *and* rest is a direct challenge to all of this: it
dignifies labor, refuses to worship it, and insists that even God rested.

## What Scripture says

Work is given before the Fall — part of being made in God's image, sharing in his
creative care of the world.

<Scripture passage="Genesis 2:15" /> <Scripture passage="Genesis 1:28" />

The Fall makes work toilsome, but does not make it evil; we are still to work
heartily, as for the Lord.

<Scripture passage="Genesis 3:17-19" /> <Scripture passage="Colossians 3:23-24" />

God himself rested, and built rest into the very structure of the week as a holy
gift for all — including servants and animals.

<Scripture passage="Genesis 2:2-3" /> <Scripture passage="Exodus 20:8-11" />

Jesus frees the Sabbath from legalism: it is made *for* us, a mercy, not a
burden — and he invites the weary to find their deepest rest in him.

<Scripture passage="Mark 2:27" /> <Scripture passage="Matthew 11:28-30" />

And Scripture insists on justice for workers — the laborer deserves fair and
prompt wages (connecting to [Wealth & Poverty](/issues/wealth-and-poverty)).

<Scripture passage="James 5:4" /> <Scripture passage="Deuteronomy 24:14-15" />

## Discern

Christians agree work is good and rest is a gift; they differ on how to keep
Sabbath today and how tightly to tie identity to vocation.

<Views title="How should Christians keep Sabbath now?">
<View label="A set day of rest" open>

Keep a weekly day genuinely set apart — from work, commerce, and hurry — as a
discipline and a witness. *Caution:* guard against a new legalism; the Sabbath
was made for us, not us for it.

</View>
<View label="A rhythm of rest">

The principle matters more than a particular day: build real, regular rhythms of
rest, worship, and unplugging into life, especially when schedules are
irregular. *Caution:* "flexibility" can quietly become "never actually resting."

</View>
<View label="Rest in Christ">

The deepest Sabbath is found in Christ, who gives rest to the soul; outward rest
flows from trusting him rather than our own striving. *Caution:* inner rest is
not an excuse to skip the embodied, practical rest our bodies and families
need.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip The question rest asks
To stop working — to sleep, to keep Sabbath — is to confess that the world does
not depend on us, and that we are loved before we are useful. *What would it mean
to believe your worth is given by God, not earned by your output?* Burnout often
begins where that truth is forgotten.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="Is work a result of the Fall, or a good thing?">

Work is good — given *before* the Fall, when God placed humanity in the garden
"to work it and take care of it" (<Scripture passage="Genesis 2:15" />). It is
part of bearing God's image and sharing in his creative care of the world. The
Fall made work *toilsome* (<Scripture passage="Genesis 3:17-19" />), but did not
make it evil; honest labor of every kind has dignity and can be done "as for the
Lord."

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="Why is rest a command and not just a luxury?">

Because rest declares a truth we constantly forget: we are creatures, not God,
and the world is upheld by him, not by our striving. God built Sabbath into the
week and even rested himself (<Scripture passage="Genesis 2:2-3" />). Jesus said
the Sabbath was made *for* us (<Scripture passage="Mark 2:27" />) — a mercy that
frees us from the idolatry of work and the lie that our worth is our output.

</SelfCheck>
