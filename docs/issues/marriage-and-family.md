# 20 · Marriage, Family &amp; Singleness

<p class="epigraph">"God sets the lonely in families…" — Psalm 68:6</p>

The family is one of God's oldest and best gifts — and one of our most reliable
idols. Scripture honors marriage as a covenant and children as a blessing, yet it
also refuses to let the family become ultimate, insisting that the deepest family
is the **household of God**, and that the single, the widowed, and the childless
belong every bit as fully as the married parent. Get this balance wrong in either
direction and someone gets crushed: either the lonely are treated as second-class,
or the covenant of marriage is treated as disposable.

This chapter honors **marriage** as a covenant to be kept, takes seriously the
pain of its **breakdown**, dignifies **singleness** as a full calling, and calls
the Church to be the family that takes in everyone the world leaves alone. (For
the debated questions about sexuality and same-sex marriage, see
[Chapter 13](/issues/sexuality); this chapter walks the ground the whole Church
still shares.)

## Declare

<Declaration title="On marriage, family, and singleness">

We declare that **marriage is a good covenant** — faithful, self-giving, and meant
to endure — and that **singleness is an equally honored calling**, not a waiting
room or a lesser life. We declare that the Church is the **family of God**, where
the lonely are taken in and no one belongs less for being unmarried, widowed,
divorced, or childless. We renounce both the idolatry of the nuclear family and
the neglect of those without one.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 68:6" /> <Scripture passage="Mark 3:33-35" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

Two opposite pressures squeeze the modern family. On one side, the covenant of
marriage has weakened — through divorce, delay, and a culture that treats
commitment as provisional and self-fulfillment as supreme. On the other, a
loneliness epidemic is spreading: more people live alone, marry later or not at
all, and lack the thick web of family and community that earlier generations took
for granted.

The Church can mishandle both. Some church cultures so idolize the nuclear family
that they treat single, divorced, or childless adults as incomplete — even though
Jesus and Paul were single. Others drift with the culture and stop honoring the
marriage covenant at all. The gospel offers a third way: hold marriage in high
honor *and* welcome the unmarried as full members of God's family — because our
truest belonging is not biological but baptismal.

## What Scripture says

Marriage is rooted in creation as a one-flesh covenant, which Jesus treats as
serious and binding.

<Scripture passage="Genesis 2:24" /> <Scripture passage="Mark 10:6-9" />

It is finally a sign pointing to Christ's covenant love for his Church.

<Scripture passage="Ephesians 5:25-32" />

Children are a gift and a trust, to be raised in love and discipleship — and yet
Jesus relativizes even blood ties, defining his family as those who do God's
will.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 127:3" /> <Scripture passage="Mark 3:33-35" />

Singleness is honored as a genuine gift and freedom for the Lord, by both Jesus
and Paul.

<Scripture passage="Matthew 19:11-12" /> <Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 7:7-8" />

And God's people are commanded to care for those without family security — the
widow, the orphan, the lonely — for this is the heart of pure religion.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 68:5-6" /> <Scripture passage="James 1:27" />

## Discern

Christians honor marriage and singleness alike, but they differ — pastorally and
theologically — on divorce and remarriage.

<Views title="On divorce and remarriage">
<View label="Permanence emphasis" open>

Stresses Jesus' high view of the marriage covenant and limits divorce and
remarriage to narrow biblical grounds, guarding the covenant against a throwaway
culture. *Caution:* must never become cruelty toward the abused or abandoned, or
ignore Jesus' own compassion.

</View>
<View label="Grace and restoration">

Stresses God's redemption of broken situations, extending grace and the
possibility of remarriage to the divorced, especially the betrayed and abused.
*Caution:* grace must not slide into treating the covenant as disposable.

</View>
<View label="Pastoral, case-by-case">

Holds the ideal of permanence high while walking with each painful situation
individually — protecting the vulnerable (especially from abuse), naming sin
honestly, and offering real mercy. *Caution:* "it depends" must not become an
avoidance of any clear conviction at all.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip The family the gospel builds
The early Church scandalized the ancient world by becoming a *family* — calling
one another brother and sister, sharing meals and homes, taking in widows and
orphans. The cure for both the idolatry of family and the ache of loneliness is
the same: a Church that is genuinely the household of God
(<Scripture passage="Ephesians 2:19" />).
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="Does the Bible treat singleness as a lesser or incomplete life?">

No. Both Jesus and Paul were single, and Paul calls singleness a genuine gift and
a freedom for undivided devotion to the Lord
(<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 7:7-8" />). Scripture honors marriage highly,
but it locates our deepest belonging in the family of God, not in biological
family — so the single, widowed, divorced, and childless are full members, never
second-class.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="How does the Church hold together a high view of marriage and compassion for the broken?">

By refusing to choose between them. It honors marriage as a real covenant meant
to endure (<Scripture passage="Mark 10:6-9" />) — resisting a throwaway culture —
*and* it extends Christ's compassion to the divorced, the betrayed, and the
abused, protecting the vulnerable and offering grace and restoration. Neither
laxity nor cruelty reflects Jesus, who upheld the covenant and welcomed the
wounded in the same breath.

</SelfCheck>
