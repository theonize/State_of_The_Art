# 30 · Crime, Prisons &amp; Restorative Justice

<p class="epigraph">"…I was in prison and you came to visit me." — Matthew 25:36</p>

When Jesus described the final judgment, he placed himself in a startling
location: among the **imprisoned**. "I was in prison and you came to visit me."
Few people are easier for a society to forget — or to dehumanize — than those
behind bars, and few are named more directly by Christ as the place we meet him.
How a people treats its prisoners, its victims, and its offenders reveals what it
believes about justice, mercy, and the image of God.

This chapter holds together what our debates usually tear apart: God is both
**just** and **merciful**; crime has real victims who deserve justice, and
offenders remain image-bearers who can be redeemed. It asks what it would mean to
pursue a justice that does not merely punish but **restores** — making victims
whole and offenders new — without pretending evil away.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On justice and the imprisoned">

We declare that God is both **perfectly just and rich in mercy**, that **both
victims and offenders bear his image**, and that the prisoner is our neighbor —
one in whom Christ himself is met. We affirm a justice that holds real
**accountability** together with the aim of **restoration**, caring for the
wounded and seeking the redemption of the guilty. We renounce vengeance,
dehumanizing punishment, and the abandonment and forgetting of the imprisoned.

<Scripture passage="Matthew 25:35-40" /> <Scripture passage="Micah 6:8" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

Vast numbers of people are incarcerated worldwide, and in many systems prison has
become less about justice or restoration than about warehousing, punishment, and
profit — often falling hardest on the poor and the marginalized. Conditions
frequently dehumanize; reentry is set up to fail; and the formerly incarcerated
carry a lifelong mark. Meanwhile, victims of crime are too often left out of the
process entirely, their wounds unhealed even when an offender is punished.

The biblical vision is richer than either "lock them up and forget them" or "no
one is responsible for anything." Scripture takes sin and its victims with deadly
seriousness *and* refuses to reduce any person to their worst act. It holds out a
justice that restrains evil, restores victims, and seeks to redeem offenders — a
justice aimed not at vengeance but at making things right.

## What Scripture says

Christ identifies himself with the prisoner, making the visited cell a meeting
place with him.

<Scripture passage="Matthew 25:35-40" /> <Scripture passage="Hebrews 13:3" />

God is a just judge who does not clear the guilty — yet "mercy triumphs over
judgment," and he delights to redeem.

<Scripture passage="Exodus 34:6-7" /> <Scripture passage="James 2:13" />

The biblical pattern of justice often centers on **restitution** and making the
victim whole, not merely on punishment — as when Zacchaeus repays fourfold.

<Scripture passage="Exodus 22:1-6" /> <Scripture passage="Luke 19:8-10" />

Jesus announced his ministry as freedom for prisoners and release for the
oppressed, and warned us to season justice with the mercy we ourselves have
received.

<Scripture passage="Luke 4:18" /> <Scripture passage="Matthew 18:21-35" />

## Discern

Christians share the call to justice and mercy; they differ on how punishment,
restoration, and specific penalties fit together.

<Views title="How should justice be pursued?">
<View label="Restorative emphasis" open>

Aims to repair harm — making victims whole, holding offenders accountable through
restitution and changed lives, and restoring community where possible. *Caution:*
restoration must still take serious crime seriously and protect the vulnerable.

</View>
<View label="Accountability and protection">

Stresses that justice requires real consequences, the protection of society, and
the vindication of victims; mercy does not cancel responsibility. *Caution:*
accountability must not curdle into vengeance, dehumanization, or hopelessness.

</View>
<View label="On the death penalty">

Christians divide: some accept capital punishment for the gravest crimes as
legitimate justice; others oppose it, citing the sanctity of life (see
[Life & Death](/issues/life-and-death)), the risk of executing the innocent, and
the priority of mercy. *Caution:* hold this gravely, never glibly.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip The justice that remembers mercy
The parable of the unforgiving servant is aimed straight at us: we who have been
forgiven an unpayable debt are warned not to deal mercilessly with others
(<Scripture passage="Matthew 18:32-33" />). A Christian vision of justice never
forgets that the line between victim, offender, and the redeemed runs through
every human heart, including our own.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="How does the Bible hold justice and mercy together?">

It refuses to choose. God is a just judge who "does not leave the guilty
unpunished" (<Scripture passage="Exodus 34:7" />) — crime and its victims are taken
seriously — yet "mercy triumphs over judgment" (<Scripture passage="James 2:13" />),
and he delights to redeem. Biblical justice often centers on **restitution** and
restoration (Zacchaeus repaying fourfold, <Scripture passage="Luke 19:8" />), aiming
not at vengeance but at making things right and redeeming the offender.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="What is 'restorative' justice, and how does it differ from mere punishment?">

Restorative justice aims to **repair harm** — making victims whole, holding
offenders genuinely accountable (often through restitution and changed lives), and
restoring community where possible — rather than only inflicting punishment and
forgetting. It reflects the biblical emphasis on restitution and redemption. It
does not deny consequences or excuse serious evil; it insists that the goal of
justice is to set things right, not merely to retaliate.

</SelfCheck>
