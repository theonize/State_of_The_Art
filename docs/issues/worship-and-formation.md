# 24 · Worship, Formation &amp; the Consumer Church

<p class="epigraph">"…offer your bodies as a living sacrifice… Do not conform to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind." — Romans 12:1–2</p>

Here is a truth our age has nearly forgotten: **we become what we worship.** Human
beings are not primarily thinking machines who occasionally feel, but worshiping
creatures who are shaped — formed — by what we love and adore. The question is
never *whether* we are being formed, but *by what.* And while we are debating
doctrine, our phones, our feeds, and our shopping carts are quietly discipling us
all day long.

This final chapter is about the heart of the Church's life: **worship**, and the
**formation** that flows from it. It names a peculiar modern temptation — the
**consumer church**, where worship becomes a product tailored to our preferences
and church becomes one more experience to consume — and it asks what it would
take to be formed instead into the likeness of Christ.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On worship and formation">

We declare that **worship belongs to God alone** and is the beating heart of the
Church's life, and that **we are formed by what we worship**. We commit to be
shaped as disciples through Word, prayer, the table, and the body of Christ —
rather than by the idols of preference, entertainment, and consumption. We
renounce the reduction of the Church to a product and worship to a performance
staged for our tastes.

<Scripture passage="Romans 12:1-2" /> <Scripture passage="Matthew 22:37-38" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

Much of modern church life has quietly absorbed the logic of the market. Worship
is "produced," congregations are "audiences," and people "church-shop" for the
best experience — the music they like, the program for their kids, the preacher
who entertains. None of the goods here are evil; the danger is the inversion,
where worship becomes about *us* — our preferences, our felt needs, our
consumption — rather than about God.

Meanwhile, the most powerful formation in our lives often happens outside church
entirely. The "liturgies" of the screen and the store — repeated daily, aimed at
our desires — shape what we love far more than an hour on Sunday
([Chapter 10](/issues/technology) named this). If the Church does not form us
deliberately, the culture will form us by default. The recovery of God-centered
worship and intentional discipleship is therefore not nostalgia; it is survival.

## What Scripture says

We are called to offer our whole selves in worship and to be *transformed* rather
than conformed to the world's pattern.

<Scripture passage="Romans 12:1-2">
…offer your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and pleasing to God — this is your
true and proper worship. Do not conform to the pattern of this world, but be
transformed by the renewing of your mind.
</Scripture>

The first and greatest commandment is wholehearted love for God — worship is not
one activity among many but the orienting center of life.

<Scripture passage="Matthew 22:37-38" />

We become like what we worship: those who trust dead idols become as lifeless as
they are, while beholding the Lord transforms us into his image.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 115:4-8" /> <Scripture passage="2 Corinthians 3:18" />

The earliest Church was formed by concrete, shared practices — teaching, the
breaking of bread, prayer, and common life — not by consuming a product.

<Scripture passage="Acts 2:42-47" /> <Scripture passage="Hebrews 10:24-25" />

## Discern

Christians agree worship is central and formative; they differ on style and form
— and these differences need not divide.

<Views title="On worship and discipleship">
<View label="Style is secondary" open>

Whether worship is ancient or contemporary, liturgical or spontaneous matters far
less than whether it is God-centered, true, and formative. *Caution:* "worship
wars" over taste are usually the consumer mindset in disguise — the very thing to
repent of.

</View>
<View label="The shaping power of form">

Historic practices — Scripture, creeds, confession, the Lord's Supper, the
church calendar — are time-tested means of forming disciples and resisting the
culture's counter-liturgies. *Caution:* form without heart becomes empty
ritual; the goal is love, not mere routine.

</View>
<View label="Whole-life discipleship">

Formation is not confined to Sunday; it happens through daily habits, rhythms,
and community ("rules of life") that train our loves toward God. *Caution:*
personal disciplines are means to love God and neighbor, not a new
performance to perfect.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip The diagnostic question
Stop and ask: *What is actually forming me?* Count the hours and the habits — the
feeds, the ads, the notifications — against the practices meant to form you in
Christ. We are all being discipled by something. Worship is simply the decision
to be discipled by God (<Scripture passage="Romans 12:2" />).
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="What does it mean that 'we become what we worship'?">

We are worshiping creatures, shaped by what we love and adore. Scripture says
those who trust lifeless idols become as empty as they are
(<Scripture passage="Psalm 115:8" />), while those who behold the Lord are
"transformed into his image" (<Scripture passage="2 Corinthians 3:18" />).
Formation is unavoidable — the only question is *by what.* Worship directed to
God reshapes our loves toward him; misdirected worship deforms us.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="What is the danger of the 'consumer church'?">

It inverts worship — making it about *our* preferences, felt needs, and
experience rather than about God — and reduces the Church to a product to be
shopped and consumed. Meanwhile the real formation happens elsewhere, through the
daily "liturgies" of screen and store. The gospel calls us to be *transformed*,
not *conformed* (<Scripture passage="Romans 12:2" />) — formed by Word, prayer,
the table, and the body of Christ, not by the market.

</SelfCheck>
