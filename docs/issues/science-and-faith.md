# 23 · Science, Faith &amp; Wonder

<p class="epigraph">"The heavens declare the glory of God; the skies proclaim the work of his hands." — Psalm 19:1</p>

A tired story says that science and faith are locked in war, and that to gain one
you must surrender the other. It is a story that serves certain agendas — the
militant atheist's and the fearful fundamentalist's alike — but it is bad
history and worse theology. The same conviction that God made an orderly,
intelligible world actually helped *give birth* to modern science, and many of
its founders and practitioners have been people of deep faith.

This chapter clears away the false war. It distinguishes **science** (a good gift
for reading God's world) from **scientism** (the philosophical claim that science
is the *only* path to truth), presents fairly the range of faithful Christian
views on contested questions like origins, and recovers something both labs and
sanctuaries can share: **wonder**.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On faith and science">

We declare that **God is the Creator of all things**, that **all truth is God's
truth**, and that genuine science — the careful study of the world God made — is
no enemy of faith but can be a form of worship. We renounce **scientism**, the
faith-claim that science alone can tell us what is real, and we renounce the
fearful anti-intellectualism that treats honest inquiry as a threat. We receive
creation with wonder and gratitude.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 19:1" /> <Scripture passage="Colossians 1:16-17" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

Popular culture still rehearses the science-versus-religion duel — new-atheist
polemics on one side, suspicion of scientists on the other — and real damage
results: believers who think they must choose between honesty and faith, and
skeptics who think Christianity requires checking your brain at the door. Mistrust
of science has real costs; so does a faith too brittle to ask hard questions.

The deeper issue is a category confusion. **Science** is superb at describing
*how* the physical world works; it is mute on *why* there is something rather than
nothing, what a human being is *for*, whether anything is *good*. When science is
inflated into **scientism** — the dogma that only what can be measured is real —
it quietly smuggles in a whole philosophy and then forgets it did so. Christians
can love science precisely because they refuse to make it a god.

## What Scripture says

Creation itself is a kind of revelation, declaring God's glory and power.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 19:1-4" /> <Scripture passage="Romans 1:20" />

All things were made through Christ and hold together in him — so to study the
world is to trace the work of his hands.

<Scripture passage="Colossians 1:16-17" /> <Scripture passage="John 1:3" />

The same God authored the "book" of Scripture and the "book" of nature; we are
invited to search things out, for it is the glory of God to conceal a matter and
the glory of kings to search it out.

<Scripture passage="Proverbs 25:2" />

And the proper response to the vastness and intricacy of creation is not pride
but humbled wonder — as God's own answer to Job makes plain.

<Scripture passage="Job 38:1-7" /> <Scripture passage="Psalm 8:3-4" />

## Discern

Christians united in confessing God as Creator have long differed on *how* and
*when* he created. These are matters for humility, not tests of faith.

<Views title="On creation and origins">
<View label="Young-earth creation" open>

Reads Genesis 1 as a roughly literal, recent six-day creation, prioritizing a
particular reading of the text. *Caution:* should engage the scientific evidence
honestly and avoid making this reading a test of orthodoxy.

</View>
<View label="Old-earth creation">

Accepts an ancient universe and earth while seeing God's special action in
creation, reading Genesis's "days" less literally. *Caution:* let the text speak
on its own terms, not merely bend it to current science.

</View>
<View label="Evolutionary creation">

Holds that God created through evolutionary processes, which describe the *how*
of a creation God authors and sustains. *Caution:* must safeguard what Scripture
declares essential — God as Creator, and humans as image-bearers, not cosmic
accidents.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip What is essential, and what is not
Christians may hold any of the above and love the same Lord. What the creeds
actually confess is that **God made heaven and earth** and that **humanity bears
his image** — not a particular mechanism or timetable. Don't break fellowship
over the *how* (see [Church Unity](/issues/church-unity)); don't surrender the
*Who*.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="What is the difference between science and 'scientism'?">

**Science** is the careful study of the physical world — superb at describing
*how* things work. **Scientism** is the philosophical claim that science is the
*only* source of real knowledge, so that meaning, morality, beauty, and God are
dismissed as unreal. That claim cannot itself be proven by science; it is a
smuggled-in faith. Christians can wholeheartedly embrace science while rejecting
scientism (<Scripture passage="Colossians 1:16-17" />).

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="Must Christians hold one particular view of creation and origins?">

No. The creeds confess that **God made heaven and earth** and that **humans bear
his image** — the *Who* and *that*, not a particular *how* or *when*. Faithful
Christians hold young-earth, old-earth, and evolutionary-creation views; these
are disputable matters, not tests of salvation. The essentials are God as
Creator and humanity's God-given dignity; the mechanism and timetable are held
with humility (<Scripture passage="Proverbs 25:2" />).

</SelfCheck>
