# 18 · War, Peace &amp; the Use of Force

<p class="epigraph">"Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God." — Matthew 5:9</p>

The Church follows a Lord who is called the **Prince of Peace**, who told Peter
to put away his sword, and who died forgiving his executioners — and yet it lives
in a world of tyrants, genocides, and aggressors, where the innocent are
slaughtered and someone must decide whether and how to stop it. Few questions
have divided sincere Christians more sharply, or more painfully, than the use of
violence.

This chapter will not pretend the tension away. It declares what the whole Church
can affirm — that Christ is our peace, that the shedding of blood is always grave,
and that we are called to be peacemakers — and then it lays out, fairly, the two
great traditions by which faithful Christians have tried to obey: **just war** and
**Christian nonviolence**. Both hate war. Both love peace. They disagree about
what love requires when peace has failed.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On peace and the use of force">

We declare that **Jesus Christ is our peace**, that peacemaking is a mark of the
children of God, and that the taking of human life is always grave — never to be
glorified or celebrated. Whatever our convictions about force, we commit to mourn
war rather than relish it, to protect the innocent, to care for its victims and
its veterans, and to resist the easy baptizing of our own nation's violence
(see [Nationalism, Kingdom & Dominion](/issues/nationalism-kingdom-dominion)).

<Scripture passage="Matthew 5:9" /> <Scripture passage="Ephesians 2:14" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

War has not gone away; it has only grown more capable of horror — from
conventional invasions to terrorism, drones, cyberwar, and weapons that could end
civilization. And the Church remains tempted, again and again, to bless the wars
of its own tribe: to wave the cross beside the flag, to dehumanize the enemy, and
to call killing holy. That temptation is its own kind of idolatry.

Against it stand two honest Christian witnesses. One says: precisely *because*
life is sacred, force may sometimes be used — reluctantly, within strict limits —
to restrain evil and defend the defenseless. The other says: precisely *because*
life is sacred and Jesus is Lord, his people renounce killing and witness to
another way. The Church has never fully resolved this. What it must refuse is the
third option both traditions reject — the casual, flag-draped enthusiasm for
violence that asks no hard questions at all.

## What Scripture says

Jesus blesses peacemakers and calls his followers to love even enemies.

<Scripture passage="Matthew 5:9" /> <Scripture passage="Matthew 5:43-44" />

He refused the sword for his own defense and rebuked Peter for drawing it.

<Scripture passage="Matthew 26:52" /> <Scripture passage="John 18:36" />

Yet Scripture also grants the governing authorities a real, limited role in
restraining evil and protecting the innocent — the "sword" of justice.

<Scripture passage="Romans 13:3-4" />

Vengeance is reserved to God, never to us; our calling is to overcome evil with
good.

<Scripture passage="Romans 12:17-21" />

And the hope toward which we live is the end of war itself, when swords are beaten
into plowshares and the nations learn war no more.

<Scripture passage="Isaiah 2:4" /> <Scripture passage="Micah 4:3" />

## Discern

Here are the main traditions by which Christians have sought to be faithful. Each
is held by serious believers; each carries a caution.

<Views title="May Christians ever use force?">
<View label="Just war" open>

Force may be used only as a tragic last resort, under strict criteria: a just
cause (defending the innocent), legitimate authority, right intention,
proportional means, and reasonable hope of success — never targeting civilians.
*Caution:* the criteria are demanding and easily abused as a rubber stamp; most
wars fail them.

</View>
<View label="Christian nonviolence">

The way of the cross renounces lethal force; the Church witnesses to the
Kingdom by refusing to kill, trusting God, and pursuing active, courageous
peacemaking (the conviction of the early Church and the historic peace
churches). *Caution:* must answer honestly what love owes the victim of
aggression, not merely keep its own hands clean.

</View>
<View label="Peacemaking as vocation">

Whatever one's view of force, all are called to *make* peace actively —
reconciliation, diplomacy, relief, restraining vengeance, caring for war's
victims — not merely to debate it. *Caution:* peacemaking is costly and risky,
not passivity or the mere absence of conflict.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip A test against the war-fever
The surest sign the Church has lost its way is when it celebrates killing and
hates the enemy God commands it to love (<Scripture passage="Matthew 5:44" />).
Whether you hold to just war or to nonviolence, you should grieve every war, doubt
your own side's propaganda, and pray for your enemies by name.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="What do the just-war and nonviolence traditions agree on?">

A great deal: that Jesus is the Prince of Peace, that human life is sacred, that
vengeance belongs to God, that we must love our enemies, and that war is always a
tragedy to be mourned, never glorified. They differ on one hard question —
whether love may ever take up force to defend the innocent — but both reject the
flag-draped enthusiasm for violence that asks no questions
(<Scripture passage="Matthew 5:9" />).

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="What are the basic criteria of just-war thinking?">

That force may be used only as a last resort and only when it meets strict tests:
a just cause (typically defending the innocent against aggression), legitimate
authority, right intention, proportional means, reasonable hope of success, and
the protection of non-combatants. The criteria exist not to justify war easily
but to *restrain* it — and most wars, measured honestly against them, fall short.

</SelfCheck>
