# 17 · Suffering, Mental Health &amp; Lament

<p class="epigraph">"Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest." — Matthew 11:28</p>

Sooner or later, suffering finds everyone — and for many it never fully leaves.
Grief, depression, anxiety, chronic pain, trauma, loneliness: these are not the
exceptions to life but, for great numbers of people, its daily weather. The
question is not whether the people in our pews are suffering. They are. The
question is whether the Church will be a **refuge** for them or one more place
they must pretend to be fine.

The Bible never pretends. A third of its songbook is grief; its heroes break
down; its Savior weeps and sweats blood and cries out, *My God, my God, why have
you forsaken me?* A faith that cannot make room for that anguish is not the faith
of the Bible. This chapter is about recovering the lost language of **lament**,
taking **mental health** seriously, and becoming the kind of community where the
wounded are carried rather than shamed.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On suffering and the wounded">

We declare that **suffering is real, and God is near to the suffering** — close
to the brokenhearted, not far from them. We affirm that **mental illness is not a
moral failure or a lack of faith**, and that seeking help is not unbelief. We
commit to make the Church a refuge and not a courtroom for the hurting — to weep
with those who weep — and we renounce the stigma, the easy platitudes, and the
toxic positivity that drive the suffering into hiding.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 34:18" /> <Scripture passage="Romans 12:15" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

The world is in the midst of a mental-health crisis: rising anxiety, depression,
loneliness, addiction, and suicide, alongside the ordinary, crushing weight of
grief and trauma that has always been with us. Technology and isolation have
deepened it; the pace of life rarely lets the soul catch up.

The Church's record here is painfully mixed. At its best, it has been a hospital
for the soul — a people who sit in the ashes with the grieving and refuse to let
anyone suffer alone. At its worst, it has spiritualized pain ("you just need more
faith"), shamed the mentally ill, and demanded smiles where God invited tears.
The way forward is not to choose between prayer and care, but to offer both — to
take seriously both the soul and the body God made, and to be present where
answers run out.

## What Scripture says

God does not despise our grief; he draws near to it.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 34:18">
The LORD is close to the brokenhearted and saves those who are crushed in spirit.
</Scripture>

Scripture gives us permission to **lament** — to bring God our raw complaint, as
the Psalms and Lamentations do, and as Jesus himself did from the cross.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 13:1-2" /> <Scripture passage="Matthew 27:46" />

Even the great servants of God despaired — Elijah begged to die, and Paul was
"under great pressure, far beyond our ability to endure" — yet God met them with
gentleness, not condemnation.

<Scripture passage="1 Kings 19:3-9" /> <Scripture passage="2 Corinthians 1:8-9" />

We are commanded to carry one another, weeping with those who weep, and casting
our anxieties on the God who cares.

<Scripture passage="Galatians 6:2" /> <Scripture passage="1 Peter 5:7" />

And our hope is not denial but resurrection: a God who suffered with us, and who
promises an end to every tear — without pretending the tears are not real now.

<Scripture passage="Revelation 21:4" /> <Scripture passage="Isaiah 53:3-4" />

## Discern

Christians agree God is near the suffering; they differ on how spiritual care and
professional treatment fit together, and on the ancient question of *why* God
permits suffering at all.

<Views title="How do faith and mental-health care fit together?">
<View label="Holistic care" open>

We are body and soul together, so prayer, community, medicine, and counseling are
allies, not rivals; God heals through means. *Caution:* don't reduce the soul to
chemistry, or treat every struggle as merely medical.

</View>
<View label="Spiritual emphasis">

Stresses prayer, Scripture, confession, and the ministry of the Church as primary,
wary of over-medicalizing the soul. *Caution:* must never shame people away from
real medical help they need; "pray harder" can be cruel counsel.

</View>
<View label="On the mystery of suffering">

Why God allows suffering has been answered through free will, soul-making, the
reality of a fallen world, and — most deeply — the cross, where God suffers *with*
and *for* us. *Caution:* with Job, we hold these humbly; the sufferer needs
presence more than a tidy theory.

</View>
</Views>

::: warning If you are in the dark right now
If you are overwhelmed, despairing, or thinking of ending your life, please do
not carry it alone. Tell someone you trust today, reach out to a doctor or a
crisis line in your country, and let your church family help carry the weight.
Needing help is not weakness or faithlessness — it is how God made us, to bear
one another's burdens (<Scripture passage="Galatians 6:2" />). You are not a
burden; you are loved.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="Is mental illness a sign of weak faith or sin?">

No. Scripture shows faithful people — Job, the psalmists, Elijah, even Paul —
sinking under anguish and despair, and God meeting them with gentleness, not
blame (<Scripture passage="1 Kings 19:3-9" />). We are embodied souls in a fallen
world; depression and anxiety are real afflictions, not moral failures. Seeking
help — spiritual *and* medical — is wisdom, not unbelief.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="What is lament, and why does the Church need it?">

Lament is honest grief brought directly to God — complaint, questions, even "how
long?" and "why?" — modeled in roughly a third of the Psalms and on the lips of
Jesus from the cross (<Scripture passage="Matthew 27:46" />). The Church needs it
because a faith that only permits praise forces the suffering to lie. Lament lets
us be truthful *and* faithful at once, bringing our real pain to a God who can
bear it.

</SelfCheck>
