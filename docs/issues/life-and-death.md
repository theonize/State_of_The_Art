# 14 · Life &amp; Death: Bioethics at the Edges

<p class="epigraph">"…I have set before you life and death, blessings and curses. Now choose life." — Deuteronomy 30:19</p>

The most basic question any society answers — usually without noticing — is
*whose life counts?* New powers over the beginning and end of life have made that
question unavoidable. We can now screen, select, and end life at its earliest
stages; we can prolong it far past what earlier generations could imagine; and,
increasingly, we can choose to end it on request. These are not merely medical
questions. They are questions about the [image of God](/foundations/image-of-god)
and the dignity of the weakest among us.

This chapter declares what the broad Christian tradition has always held — that
**every human life, from conception to natural death, bears God's image and an
inviolable dignity** — and then discerns, with compassion, the genuinely
agonizing decisions that real people face at life's edges. It does so knowing
that many readers carry private grief here. The gospel comes to such grief not
with a verdict but with mercy.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On the sanctity of life">

We declare that **every human life bears the image of God and an inviolable
dignity — from conception to natural death** — and that this dignity does not
depend on age, ability, usefulness, or anyone's permission. We renounce the
taking of innocent life and the abandonment of the suffering, and we commit to a
**whole-life** care: protecting the unborn *and* supporting mothers, children,
the poor, the disabled, and the dying.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 139:13-16" /> <Scripture passage="Deuteronomy 30:19" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

Modern medicine is a gift that has also handed us godlike powers and godlike
dilemmas. At the beginning of life, abortion remains one of the most divisive
issues in the world, prenatal screening increasingly filters out those deemed
imperfect (the population of people with Down syndrome is falling in many
countries), and reproductive technology raises new questions about embryos and
parenthood. At the end of life, assisted suicide and euthanasia are spreading,
sometimes extended from the terminally ill to the disabled, the mentally ill,
and the simply weary of life.

Running beneath all of it is a seductive logic: that a life's worth depends on
its **quality, capacity, or wantedness**. Scripture refuses that logic at every
turn. Worth is *given* by the Creator, not earned by the creature — which is
precisely why the unborn, the disabled, and the dying are our full equals, owed
not efficiency but love.

## What Scripture says

God knows and forms us before birth; the unborn are persons to him, not
potential persons.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 139:13-16">
For you created my inmost being; you knit me together in my mother's womb… your
eyes saw my unformed body; all the days ordained for me were written in your
book before one of them came to be.
</Scripture>

He calls and consecrates before birth, and even an unborn child can leap for joy.

<Scripture passage="Jeremiah 1:5" /> <Scripture passage="Luke 1:41-44" />

Life and death belong to God, who gives and takes away — so we are stewards of
life, not its masters.

<Scripture passage="Job 1:21" /> <Scripture passage="Deuteronomy 32:39" />

Yet the God of life is also near to the suffering and the dying; he does not
despise the broken, and he promises that death itself will be undone.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 34:18" /> <Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 15:54-57" />

## Discern

All these affirm the dignity of life; the hard questions are *how* to honor it in
tragic, complex situations — and how law and medicine should respond.

<Views title="At the beginning of life">
<View label="Legal protection emphasis" open>

Stresses that the unborn are our weakest neighbors and deserve the law's
protection, as any vulnerable person does. *Caution:* protection must be matched
by real, generous support for mothers and children, or it rings hollow.

</View>
<View label="Support and care emphasis">

Stresses reducing the desperation behind most abortions — poverty, abandonment,
fear — through tangible support for women, adoption, and families. *Caution:*
compassion for women and conviction about the unborn are not rivals; the
whole-life ethic holds both.

</View>
<View label="The hardest cases">

Most traditions recognize genuinely tragic situations (such as a grave threat to
the mother's life) that call for sober, prayerful medical and pastoral wisdom
rather than slogans. *Caution:* hard cases are real but rare, and must not be
used to erase the dignity of the unborn in general.

</View>
</Views>

<Views title="At the end of life">
<View label="Care, not killing" open>

The historic Christian conviction: we may not intend the death of the innocent,
including ourselves. The answer to suffering is presence, pain relief, and
hospice — not euthanasia. *Caution:* this must be paired with real investment in
palliative care so "do not kill" is not heard as "do not help."

</View>
<View label="Allowing natural death">

There is a real moral difference between *killing* and *accepting death*:
declining futile, burdensome treatment and allowing a natural death is not
euthanasia, and need not be resisted at all costs. *Caution:* this discernment
requires wisdom and good counsel, not a blanket rule either way.

</View>
<View label="Accompaniment">

Whatever the medical decisions, the Church's first calling is to *be present* —
to refuse to let anyone die alone, afraid, or feeling like a burden.
*Caution:* presence is not a substitute for moral clarity, but moral clarity
without presence is cruelty.

</View>
</Views>

::: warning A word to the grieving
If you have had an abortion, lost a child, or made an impossible end-of-life
decision, hear this first: there is no sin beyond the reach of the cross, and no
grief God will not meet with mercy (<Scripture passage="Psalm 34:18" />). This
chapter is not a tribunal. It is a call to build a Church that protects life
*and* embraces the broken.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="On what does human dignity depend, according to Scripture?">

On God, not on us. We are made in his image and knit together by him in the womb
(<Scripture passage="Psalm 139:13-16" />), so worth is *given* by the Creator,
not earned by capacity, age, or usefulness. That is exactly why the unborn, the
disabled, and the dying possess full and equal dignity — the "quality of life"
logic that ranks lives is foreign to the gospel.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="Is declining futile medical treatment the same as euthanasia?">

No. There is a real moral difference between *intending to kill* and *accepting
death*. Euthanasia and assisted suicide aim at death as the means to end
suffering; declining burdensome, futile treatment accepts that death is coming
and refuses to prolong dying at all costs. The first the Christian tradition
rejects; the second can be a faithful, peaceful acceptance of mortality — made
with wisdom, prayer, and good counsel.

</SelfCheck>
