# 31 · Beauty, the Arts &amp; Imagination

<p class="epigraph">"…whatever is lovely… think about such things." — Philippians 4:8</p>

We end where creation began — with beauty. The God who made the world did not
make it merely functional; he made it *glorious*, lavishing color, song, and form
on a cosmos that did not need to be lovely to work. Beauty is not decoration on
the edges of the faith; it runs to the heart of who God is. And yet the modern
world has grown utilitarian and disenchanted, reducing worth to usefulness and
flattening wonder into a scroll — while parts of the Church have treated art with
suspicion, as a frivolous distraction from "real" ministry.

This final chapter recovers beauty as a serious concern of the people of God: the
Lord as the first **Artist**, the Spirit as the giver of artistic gifts,
**imagination** as a gift to be sanctified, and the arts as both **worship** and
**witness** in a world starving for the transcendent.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On beauty and the arts">

We declare that **God is the source of all beauty** — himself beautiful, and the
maker of a beautiful world — and that the artistic gifts of his people are given
by his Spirit for his glory and the good of all. We affirm that **beauty matters**,
pointing beyond itself to God, and that the Church is called to make and steward
it. We renounce both the **idolatry** that makes art ultimate and the
**utilitarian contempt** that dismisses beauty as a luxury.

<Scripture passage="Philippians 4:8" /> <Scripture passage="Exodus 31:1-5" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

Our age is, in a peculiar way, both saturated with images and starved of beauty.
Screens flood us with content engineered to capture attention, while genuine
beauty — the kind that stops us, humbles us, and points beyond itself — grows
scarce in a culture that prizes the useful, the fast, and the cheap. People feel
the loss as a kind of disenchantment, a flatness, a hunger they cannot name.

The Church has a glorious heritage here — the Psalms, cathedrals, sacred music,
illuminated manuscripts, poetry, and story that have carried the gospel and
lifted hearts for millennia. But it has also, at times, grown philistine:
suspicious of art, satisfied with the shoddy and sentimental, treating beauty as
optional or even dangerous. To recover beauty is not to retreat from mission; in
a disenchanted age, beauty may be one of the most powerful witnesses the Church
has — an argument that slips past our defenses and awakens longing for God.

## What Scripture says

We are told to fill our minds with whatever is true, noble, and **lovely** —
beauty is a fit object of Christian attention.

<Scripture passage="Philippians 4:8" />

God himself fills artists with his Spirit, gifting skill and craft for beautiful
work — the first person said to be "filled with the Spirit" in Scripture is an
*artist*.

<Scripture passage="Exodus 31:1-5" /> <Scripture passage="Exodus 35:30-35" />

Creation's beauty is a form of speech, declaring God's glory wordlessly to all.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 19:1-4" /> <Scripture passage="Psalm 96:9" />

And worship in Scripture overflows with art — poetry, music, song, and skilled
craft offered to God.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 150:1-6" /> <Scripture passage="1 Chronicles 15:16" />

## Discern

Christians agree beauty matters; they emphasize the place and purpose of the arts
differently.

<Views title="What is the place of the arts?">
<View label="Art in service of worship" open>

The arts are gifts chiefly to enrich worship and carry the gospel — music,
poetry, image, and story that lift hearts to God and proclaim his truth.
*Caution:* don't reduce all art to utility; not every work must "say" something
explicit to glorify God.

</View>
<View label="Art as good in itself">

Beauty and creativity are goods in their own right, part of bearing God's image
and a form of common grace to be celebrated wherever it is found. *Caution:*
beauty is a signpost to God, not a substitute for him — guard against making art
an idol.

</View>
<View label="Art as cultural witness">

Excellent, honest art is a powerful witness in a disenchanted age — awakening
longing, telling the truth about the world, and earning a hearing for the gospel.
*Caution:* witness through quality and truth, not propaganda or sentimentality.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip The argument that slips past our defenses
In a skeptical age, an argument can be resisted, but beauty disarms. A piece of
music, a painting, a story, a life made lovely by grace can awaken a longing for
God that no debate could — which is why the Church's care for beauty is not a
distraction from its mission but a part of it (<Scripture passage="Psalm 27:4" />).
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="Why should the Church care about beauty and the arts?">

Because beauty runs to the heart of who God is: he is beautiful, and he made a
glorious world that did not need to be lovely to function. He fills artists with
his Spirit (<Scripture passage="Exodus 31:1-5" />), creation's beauty declares his
glory (<Scripture passage="Psalm 19:1" />), and Scripture's worship overflows with
art. We are told to dwell on "whatever is lovely" (<Scripture passage="Philippians 4:8" />).
Beauty is no luxury; it points beyond itself to God and, in a disenchanted age,
witnesses powerfully to him.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="What are the two errors to avoid regarding art?">

**Idolatry** — treating art or beauty as ultimate, a substitute for God — and
**utilitarian contempt** — dismissing beauty as frivolous because it is not
"useful." Scripture steers between them: beauty is a real good and a gift of the
Spirit, to be made and enjoyed for God's glory, yet always a signpost to the
Creator rather than a rival to him.

</SelfCheck>
