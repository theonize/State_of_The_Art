# 29 · Aging, Honor &amp; the Elderly

<p class="epigraph">"Stand up in the presence of the aged, show respect for the elderly, and revere your God." — Leviticus 19:32</p>

We live in a civilization that worships youth, beauty, and productivity — and so
quietly discards the old. As people age out of usefulness by the culture's
measure, they are too often warehoused, isolated, and forgotten, their wisdom
unheard and their loneliness unnoticed. Against this, Scripture commands something
startling: **stand up** in the presence of the aged. Honor them. See in gray hair
not decline but a crown.

This chapter declares that the elderly bear God's image and a special honor, that
their worth — like everyone's — is given by God and not earned by output, and
that the Church is meant to be an **intergenerational family** in which the old
are treasured, cared for, and heard, faithful to the end.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On aging and the elderly">

We declare that **the elderly fully bear God's image** and are owed honor, that
their **worth is not their productivity** but is given by God, and that their long
faith and wisdom are a gift to the whole Church. We commit to honor, include, and
care for the aged — and for our own aging parents — and to be an intergenerational
family where none are discarded. We renounce the ageism and neglect that warehouse
and forget the old.

<Scripture passage="Leviticus 19:32" /> <Scripture passage="Proverbs 16:31" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

The world is aging fast, and modern life has unbundled the generations — the old
increasingly live apart, cared for (when they are cared for) by institutions
rather than woven into family and community. Loneliness among the elderly has
become an epidemic; many spend their final years feeling useless and unseen. A
culture that measures worth by productivity has little place for those it deems
past their usefulness — a logic that also fuels pressure at the end of life (see
[Life & Death](/issues/life-and-death)).

The Church should look starkly different. In Scripture, age brings honor, not
disposal; elders are sources of wisdom and blessing; and God's people are
commanded to care for aging parents as an expression of true godliness. A Church
that reflects this — where the generations actually know and need one another —
is a quiet, powerful witness against the throwaway spirit of the age.

## What Scripture says

Honoring the elderly is tied directly to revering God.

<Scripture passage="Leviticus 19:32" />

Long life and gray hair are framed as a crown and a blessing, and wisdom is
expected to dwell with age.

<Scripture passage="Proverbs 16:31" /> <Scripture passage="Job 12:12" />

Caring for aging parents and relatives is not optional but a mark of genuine
faith — to neglect it is to deny the faith.

<Scripture passage="1 Timothy 5:3-8" /> <Scripture passage="Mark 7:9-13" />

The faithful aged are pictured still bearing fruit, and the psalmist prays not to
be cast off when strength fails — a prayer God honors.

<Scripture passage="Psalm 92:12-15" /> <Scripture passage="Psalm 71:18" />

## Discern

Christians agree the elderly must be honored; they weigh differently how the
duties of care are best shared.

<Views title="How should we care for the aging?">
<View label="Family responsibility" open>

Scripture lays the first duty on the family to honor and provide for aging
parents and relatives. *Caution:* family duty must be matched with real support,
so caregivers are not crushed and the family-less are not abandoned.

</View>
<View label="Church and community">

The household of God shares the load — visiting, including, and caring for
elderly members, especially those without families (see
[Marriage, Family & Singleness](/issues/marriage-and-family)). *Caution:* the
church's role supplements rather than replaces family and just structures.

</View>
<View label="Good institutional care">

Skilled care (in homes or facilities) can be a faithful act of love when done
with dignity and continued presence — not a way to outsource and forget.
*Caution:* never let "they're being cared for" become an excuse to stop showing
up.

</View>
</Views>

::: tip Worth that does not retire
The deepest gift the Church gives the elderly is the same it gives everyone: the
truth that their worth was never their usefulness. The God who made them does not
cast them off when they are old and gray (<Scripture passage="Psalm 71:18" />) —
and neither should his people. To honor the aged is to rehearse the gospel of
grace.
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="How does the Bible's view of aging differ from the culture's?">

The culture prizes youth and productivity and tends to discard those it deems no
longer useful. Scripture commands the opposite: "stand up in the presence of the
aged" (<Scripture passage="Leviticus 19:32" />), calls gray hair "a crown of
splendor" (<Scripture passage="Proverbs 16:31" />), expects wisdom to come with
age, and pictures the faithful elderly still bearing fruit
(<Scripture passage="Psalm 92:14" />). Worth is given by God, not earned by
output — so the old are to be honored, not warehoused.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="Whose responsibility is the care of the elderly?">

Scripture lays the first duty on the **family** — providing for aging parents and
relatives is a mark of genuine faith, and neglecting it is "to deny the faith"
(<Scripture passage="1 Timothy 5:8" />). But the **household of God** shares the
load, especially for those without family, and good institutional care can be a
faithful act of love when joined to continued presence. The point throughout:
honor and presence, never outsourcing and forgetting.

</SelfCheck>
