# 25 · Women, Men &amp; Ministry

<p class="epigraph">"There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus." — Galatians 3:28</p>

Wherever the gospel has gone, it has lifted the status of women — and wherever the
Church has been unfaithful, it has too often demeaned, silenced, or used them.
That double history sits behind one of the most heated debates in the Church
today: not whether women have equal worth (they unmistakably do), but how their
gifts and callings relate to leadership in the church and the home.

Following the book's method, this chapter **declares** what the whole Church can
affirm — the equal dignity and Spirit-given giftedness of women, and the sin of
demeaning or abusing them — and then **discerns** honestly between the two main
faithful positions, **complementarian** and **egalitarian**, held by sincere
Christians who equally love Scripture.

## Declare

<Declaration title="On women and men">

We declare that **women and men equally bear God's image** and equal dignity, that
**women are gifted and called by the Spirit** and indispensable to the Church's
life and mission, and that the gospel honors them. We renounce every demeaning,
silencing, abuse, and exclusion of women from the dignity and respect they are
owed, and the long history of treating them as lesser. On *this* the Church must
not be divided.

<Scripture passage="Galatians 3:28" /> <Scripture passage="Acts 2:17-18" />

</Declaration>

## Where we are

Around the world, women are the backbone of the Church — the majority of its
worshipers, its most tireless servants, its first missionaries to their own
families. And yet women have also been kept from using their gifts, talked over,
blamed, and in too many places abused by the very leaders meant to protect them.
Any conversation about "roles" that forgets this history will ring hollow.

The sharp debate among faithful Christians is narrower than the shouting suggests.
It is not about whether women are equal, intelligent, gifted, or called — all
agree they are. It is about how to read a handful of biblical texts on leadership
and order in the church and home. Sincere, Scripture-honoring believers have
landed in two main places, and it is possible to hold one's own conviction firmly
while honoring those who differ.

## What Scripture says

Jesus radically dignified women in a culture that did not — teaching them,
receiving their support, and making them the **first witnesses** of the
resurrection.

<Scripture passage="Luke 8:1-3" /> <Scripture passage="John 20:16-18" />

The Spirit is poured out on sons *and* daughters, who prophesy; and women labored
as leaders and coworkers across the early Church.

<Scripture passage="Acts 2:17-18" /> <Scripture passage="Romans 16:1-7" />

Paul can both affirm women praying and prophesying and write the much-debated
texts about teaching, authority, and order.

<Scripture passage="1 Corinthians 11:5" /> <Scripture passage="1 Timothy 2:11-12" />

And he sets the tone for the home in mutual submission and sacrificial,
Christlike love — not domination.

<Scripture passage="Ephesians 5:21-25" />

## Discern

Here are the two main faithful positions. Each is held by serious, Bible-loving
Christians; each carries a caution.

<Views title="On women in leadership">
<View label="Complementarian" open>

Men and women are equal in worth and dignity but called to some distinct,
complementary roles, with certain teaching/eldership offices reserved for men,
appealing to creation order and texts like 1 Timothy 2. *Caution:* must never
slide into devaluing women, excluding their gifts more broadly, or excusing male
domination and abuse.

</View>
<View label="Egalitarian">

The Spirit gives gifts and callings without regard to gender, so women may serve
in every role including eldership and preaching, appealing to Galatians 3:28,
the many women leaders in Scripture, and a reading of the debated texts as
context-specific. *Caution:* must take the whole of Scripture seriously, not set
texts against each other to fit the culture.

</View>
<View label="Held without contempt">

Whatever one concludes, both sides must reject the abuse and demeaning of women,
honor their gifts, and refuse to treat this disputable matter as a test of who
truly follows Christ. *Caution:* firmness of conviction and charity toward those
who differ belong together.

</View>
</Views>

::: warning Where there is no debate
The debate is about *roles*, never about *worth*. Wherever "headship" or
"order" has been used to silence, control, belittle, or abuse women, that is not
a faithful position in this debate — it is sin, and it contradicts the Christ who
honored women and the love that "does not insist on its own way"
(<Scripture passage="Ephesians 5:25" />).
:::

## Self-check

<SelfCheck q="What do complementarian and egalitarian Christians agree on?">

That women and men equally bear God's image and dignity; that women are gifted
and called by the Spirit and vital to the Church; and that demeaning, silencing,
or abusing women is sin (<Scripture passage="Galatians 3:28" />). The
disagreement is not about women's worth or gifting but about how to read specific
texts on leadership roles in church and home — a disputable matter, not a test of
salvation.

</SelfCheck>

<SelfCheck q="Is the question of women's roles a gospel essential to divide over?">

No. Faithful, Scripture-honoring Christians hold both the complementarian and
egalitarian positions; it is a genuinely disputable matter, to be held with
conviction *and* charity. What is *not* disputable is the equal dignity of women
and the sinfulness of demeaning or abusing them — there the Church must speak with
one voice (<Scripture passage="Ephesians 5:21" />).

</SelfCheck>
